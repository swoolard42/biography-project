John Adams
A Founding Father of the United States
1735 – 1826

“A government of laws, and not of men.”
– John Adams

John Adams was born in Braintree, MA on October 30th, 1735. He was a career politician and a founding father of the United States of America. He started as a lawyer in the Boston area. After the Boston Massacre, Adams agreed to defend the British soldiers in the trial. This made him unpopular, but showed is honesty and determination to uphold truth and facts. He was later called to join the Continental Congress and helped oversee the creation of the Declaration of Independence, written by Thomas Jefferson. During the Revolutionary War, Adams went overseas and joined Ben Franklin in Paris to ask for help. Later he became the vice president for George Washington and then President himself in 1796. He is most known for passing the Alien and Sedition Acts during his presidency. He later lost the next election to his political rival Jefferson. In retirement, Adams lived out of his home Peacefield with his family. Later, he and Jefferson began to write each other letters reflecting on their rivalry and the state of the nation they helped create. they both died on July 4, 1826, 50 years after the creation of the Declaration of Independence.

Timeline

1770

The Boston Massacre

After the Boston Massacre, Adams was asked to defend the British soldiers involved in the incident in court. This would have been a very hard choice for Adams, as there had been a rising anti-Britain sentiment rising in the colonies. Despite the risk, Adams wanted to uphold the law and defended them in the trial. This exposed him an unpopular, but radical force in the colonies.

1774

Joins the Continental Congress

In the summer of 1774, Adams and his cousin Samuel are elected to be two of the 12-13 delegates to go to Philadelphia to attend the first Continental Congress. Adams and Samuel quickly became leaders of the radical faction. This portion of delegates rejected the idea of a possible reconciliation with Great Britain.

1776

Declaration of Independence

After joining the Continental Congress in 1774, Adams worked to push the agenda of American independence. During the second Congress in 1775,Adams appointed Thomas Jefferson to write the Declaration of Independence. Adams and Franklin helped Jefferson develop the document and looked over his drafts. the Declaration was released on July 4, 1776.

1778
- 1788

Diplomat in Europe

Adams was initially sent to France to work out an alliance with Paris. Franklin had already sealed the deal and Adams went home. Shortly after, Adams was sent back to Paris to negotiate an end to the war with Britain. Adams was known to be an unusual diplomat, due to his confrontational style. Later, He became the ambassador to Great Britain before returning home to serve as vice president to Washington.

1783

The Treaty of Paris

The Treaty of Paris, or more collectively called the Peace of Paris was a group of treaties ending the American Revolutionary War. As a diplomat with France. Adams along with Ben Franklin helped carry out the negotiations. Adams was noted to have some erratic behavior during this time, supposedly caused by Graves' Disease.

1797
- 1801

President of the United States

Adams won the presidency in a close race against his friend and rival, Thomas Jefferson. Sadly, Adams presidency was full of controversy due to the "quasi-war" between the two political parties at the time. This tension was caused by the question of whether to help the French during their revolution. His most well known act as president was the Alien and Sedition Act, which allowed for the removal of foreign-born people and a crackdown on antigovernmental writings in newspapers.

Additional Info

Family Home of Peacefield

Peacefield is the family home of the Adams family. It was originally built in 1731, and became the home of the Adams family from 1788-1927. It's owners included President John Adams and his wife Abagail, their son President John Quincy Adams, and more Adams family members. The grounds include a 18th century garden and an orchard.

HBO Miniseries

The life of John Adams became the topic of a HBO Miniseries released in 2008. It was directly adapted from author David McCullough's award winning biography on the man. It covers from the Boston Massacre all the way to his death on the 50th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence on July 4th, 1826. It was critically acclaimed and garnered the Primetime Emmy award for outstanding limited series and a Golden Globe for best actor for star Paul Giamatti. It is praised for depicting Adams wife Abagail, and making her a second protagonist.

Famous Family Members

Outside from John Adams himself, there are many others in the family worth knowing. Chiefly, his son John Quincy Adams went on to become the 6th President of the United States. Others include his wife Abagail, who is known to history through her countless letters between her and Adams. John's cousin, Samuel Adams was also a politician and was one of the delegates to attend the first Continental Congress.
